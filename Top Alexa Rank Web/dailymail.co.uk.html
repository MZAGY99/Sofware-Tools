The man then scrambles under to steal its catalytic converter, centre, and the pair then make off with the valuable part, right. Catalytic converter theft is soaring across the country as criminals cash in on a boom in global precious metal prices. These metals - platinum, palladium and rhodium - are used in converters to capture noxious gases as they flow through a vehicle's exhaust system. The quantities are small, but such is their value - higher than gold and silver - that they are well worth the crooks stealing. Palladium, in particular, has shot up in value this year, due to increased demand for converters from car manufacturers in China and India, which are reducing emissions to combat extreme air pollution in their big cities. The result is a bonanza for criminals, who can strip out a converter in minutes. Once stolen, the converters are offered to unscrupulous scrap-metal dealers for £200 or more a time - good money for thieves who can bag half a dozen in a day. They are then dismantled and the extracted metals sold in powder form to refineries around the world for recycling.
